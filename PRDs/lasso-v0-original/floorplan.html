1. Primary floorplan — A4-lean, 6 SRAM banks (384 KB)

SRAM macro (CF_SRAM_16384x32, 64 KB each, ~1100 × 800 µm, 0.88 mm²) 6 × 0.88 = 5.28 mm² KV Cache Engine (TurboQuant: Hadamard + codebook LUT + bit-pack) 0.09 mm² Token Importance Unit (score accumulator + comparator tree) 0.02 mm² Memory Hierarchy Controller (page table, bank arbiter) 0.03 mm² Attention Compute Unit (32-wide Q·K, compressed-domain mode) 0.06 mm² Wishbone slave + CSRs 0.02 mm² Routing channels, clock tree, cell fill — ~65% of the logic region ~0.81 mm² Power distribution ring (~100 µm thick on all four sides) ~0.50 mm²
The point: Look at the colored rectangles in the logic region. Those tiny bright squares are the actual logic cells. Even the largest block (KV Cache Engine) is 0.09 mm² out of the 10.28 mm² die — less than 1%. The whole logic family combined (0.22 mm²) is smaller than a single SRAM macro. This is why "add another block" is not an area question — it's a verification and physical-design question.

2. Zoomed logic-region detail (500× scale)

The logic region in diagram 1 is so small that the individual blocks are barely visible. Here's the same logic region zoomed in, showing what's actually inside the KV Cache Engine — the datapath that does the TurboQuant work.

12 ns total Fits 20 ns budget at 50 MHz with 8 ns margin. Throughput: 1 group of 32 elements per cycle after fill (100 MB/s at 50 MHz) No multipliers on this path! Hadamard = adders only, codebook = comparators + ROM, pack = shift+OR. Multiply-free encode is why TurboQuant fits so well in 130nm — you skip the most expensive gate class.

3. Why one slice, not many (your NVIDIA SM question)

LASSO: one 32-wide pipelined slice

NVIDIA H100: 132 SMs, each 128-wide

Why LASSO isn't built like an H100

QuestionLASSO (130nm, 8.5 mm² usable)H100 (4N, 814 mm²)
What is the workload? Streaming KV cache compress/decompress. One vector at a time from Wishbone. Dense matmul across huge matrices. Thousands of independent threads.
Primary bottleneck Wishbone bandwidth (~200 MB/s). Internal compute can't go faster than input. Memory bandwidth (HBM ~3 TB/s). Needs thousands of ALUs to saturate.
Does adding a second slice help? No. You'd double area + verification and not move throughput — input is single-stream. Yes. Work is already embarrassingly parallel across matrix rows/cols.
Where's the parallelism? 32-wide SIMD within each pipeline stage + 4-stage pipeline = 128 concurrent ops/cycle. 132 SMs × 128 cores × warp-level SIMT = 16,896 concurrent FP32 ops/cycle.
Latency per element ~12 ns (4 pipeline stages at 50 MHz). ~1–2 ns (deep warp scheduling at 1.78 GHz).
The right mental model for LASSO: You're building a narrow, deep, fully utilized pipeline. Every cycle, 32 elements enter, 32 go through Hadamard, 32 go through codebook lookup, 32 get packed, 32 go to SRAM. No cores sit idle. Adding a second slice would double everything but your input stream is already single-threaded from Wishbone — the second slice would starve. One slice is the right answer at this scale.
When would multi-slice make sense? Only if you had multiple independent input streams — e.g., if the chip had two Wishbone ports serving two different attention heads. At 130nm with one Wishbone slave, the answer is a firm no. At 28nm with a wide AXI interface and 10 GB/s sustained input, you'd add 4–8 slices. That's a v3 problem.

5. What's actually being asked when you ask "how big"

QuestionAnswer
How big is the die?2.92 × 3.52 mm = 10.28 mm² (Caravel user area, fixed)
How big is the usable area?~8.5 mm² after power ring, clock tree, unroutable corners
How big is one SRAM bank?0.875 mm² (~1100 × 800 µm, estimated)
How big is the KV Cache Engine?0.09 mm² — smaller than 1/10th of an SRAM bank
How big is ALL logic combined?0.22 mm² — about 1/4 of an SRAM bank
How many "slices" does it have?One. 32-wide SIMD + 4-stage pipeline = 128 ops/cycle. No separate cores.
Is there a register file?Pipeline registers between stages (tiny). No big register file like a CPU.
Are there multiple execution units?Within the pipeline: yes (32× Hadamard adders, 224× codebook comparators). But there's one flow.
Why not multi-slice?Input is single-stream from Wishbone. Second slice would starve. One fat pipeline is the right answer.
Where's the "working memory"?The 6 SRAM banks. That's your 384 KB of KV cache. No other memory on-chip.

If you squint at the floorplan, what you're looking at is:

  • A memory chip with a compression engine bolted on. 50%+ of the die is SRAM.
  • One wide, deep pipeline that walks each vector through Hadamard → codebook → pack → SRAM write. And the reverse on reads.
  • No instruction fetch, no decode, no scheduler, no caches, no register file, no branch predictor. It's not a CPU.
  • No warps, no SMs, no thread blocks, no shared memory regions. It's not a GPU.
  • It's a fixed-function dataflow pipeline — purpose-built for exactly one thing (KV cache compression), and it does that at a fraction of the area a general-purpose chip would need.
